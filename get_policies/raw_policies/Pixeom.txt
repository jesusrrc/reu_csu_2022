<h1 class="article__title">Pixeom Is An Anti-Corporate Cloud Device That Wants To Give You Back Your Data Privacy</h1>
</div>
<div class="article__byline-wrapper">
<div class="article__byline">
<a href="https://techcrunch.com/author/natasha-lomas/">
			Natasha Lomas		</a>
<span class="article__byline__meta">
<a href="https://twitter.com/riptari">@riptari</a>
<span class="text--green"> / </span>
						8 years		</span>
</div>
</div>
<div class="article__featured-image-wrapper breakout">
<img class="article__featured-image" src="https://techcrunch.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/pixeom.jpg?w=700"/>

<div class="article-content">
<p id="speakable-summary">Meet <a href="https://www.kickstarter.com/projects/pixeom/pixeom-the-personal-exchange-device" target="_blank">Pixeom</a>, a prototype device being Kickstarted by <span style="line-height:1.625;">brother and sister duo Karishma and Sam Nagar, with the aim of offering alternative cloud services that don’t require you to hand over the keys to your private data in exchange for a convenient digital service.  </span></p>
<p>Prior to Edward Snowden’s revelations about the <a href="https://beta.techcrunch.com/tag/nsa/">NSA</a>‘s (and <a href="https://beta.techcrunch.com/2013/11/01/britains-gchq-collaborated-with-other-eu-nations-to-enable-broad-internet-surveillance/">other governments’ security agencies</a>‘) dragnet surveillance of everyone’s online lives, Pixeom would probably have sunk without a trace. Or not even been conceived of as a mainstream project at all.</p>
<p>But, post-Snowden, there’s a perfect storm of <a href="https://beta.techcrunch.com/2013/07/04/spying-bad-for-business/">cloud-computing concerns</a> amassing to undermine the convenience of mainstream online services. And, after a month seeking crowdfunding on Kickstarter, Pixeom has passed its funding target of $50,000 — with close to 400 backers, and still has 15 days left to run on its campaign.</p>
<p>In addition to the funds it’s currently raising on Kickstarter, Pixeom has also secured more than $500,000 in private funding to get this personal cloud device to market.</p>
<p>It’s clear that Snowden has shone a light on something very rotten lurking at the core of mainstream cloud services. Sure, web mail clients are handy — and you get a chunk of free storage — but you’re also effectively handing the keys to your inbox over to the NSA (et al) should they happen to feel like dipping into your personal correspondence one lunchtime or other.</p>
<p>And of course it’s a given that your personal data will be mined by the service provider; it’s a free service, so your privacy is the product. This general snooping on/data-mining of users is a routine business model for the majority of free cloud-based services and apps.</p>
<p>Multiple big name tech giants have built hugely lucrative businesses off of selling your likes and dislikes to advertisers in exchange for furnishing you with a platform where you can share that data in the first place. Or store the personal stuff they need to peek at to gather sellable intel on you.</p>
<p>Plus there’s also the sloppy security of (<a href="https://beta.techcrunch.com/2013/12/31/hackers-claim-to-publish-list-of-4-6m-snapchat-usernames-and-numbers/">certain</a>) startups that leak your personal info all over the Internets just because they’re more interested in expanding their user-base than expending effort on adequate security measures.</p>
<p>So it goes.</p>
<p>Or so it has, but — whipped up by the storm of snooping revelations — some counter-currents are (finally) emerging to challenge the accepted wisdom that accessing the convenience of cloud services necessitates giving up your personal data and abandoning any shred of privacy in the process.</p>
<p>Projects like <a href="https://beta.techcrunch.com/2014/01/15/blackphone/">Blackphone</a> or the <a href="https://beta.techcrunch.com/2013/11/05/dark-mail/">Dark Mail Alliance</a>. And now <a href="https://www.kickstarter.com/projects/pixeom/pixeom-the-personal-exchange-device" target="_blank">Pixeom</a>. Pixeom’s makers say they want to facilitate a new type of data exchange for Internet users which is hosted on an anti-corporate cloud that sits in your own home — i.e. not somewhere in a vast datacenter in California where giant branded spiders can crawl all over it.</p>
<p>One half of the Pixeom founder team, Sam Nagar, who previously worked as a software engineer at Microsoft in Mountain View, describes the device as follows:</p>
<blockquote><p><span style="color:#686c68;font-family:alright_sansmediumitalic, HelveticaNeue, Helvetica, Arial, sans-serif;line-height:1.5;">In a nutshell: </span><br/>
<span style="color:#686c68;font-family:alright_sansmediumitalic, HelveticaNeue, Helvetica, Arial, sans-serif;line-height:1.5;">1. All of your cloud services in a box for a small one-time cost, we are a combination of Dropbox/Box/Facebook/Shopify with a focus on privacy. </span></p>
<p><span style="color:#686c68;font-family:alright_sansmediumitalic, HelveticaNeue, Helvetica, Arial, sans-serif;line-height:1.5;">2. Scalable device that continues to grow as one large storage pool instead of multiple “islands” of user data.</span></p>
<p><span style="color:#686c68;font-family:alright_sansmediumitalic, HelveticaNeue, Helvetica, Arial, sans-serif;line-height:1.5;">3. An optional exchange network that anyone can browse full of public content from people’s devices that can one day rival the large datacenter based public networks.</span></p></blockquote>
<p>What exactly is Pixeom? It’s a personal, encrypted cloud device that plugs into your home Internet via Ethernet and provides cloud storage for your digital content without having to go through any third party intermediaries, and with support for unlimited expansion of said storage — either by chaining together multiple Pixeoms, or by plugging in additional USB hard drives.</p>
<p>The device is not just focused on storage though. The Pixeom platform also offers alternative cloud apps to replace online services — name-checking the likes of Dropbox, Box, Google Drive, Microsoft Sharepoint, Facebook and Amazon as services it wants to offer an alternative to — in a simpler (and potentially cheaper) way than by having to set up a NAS box or host your own server.</p>
<p>The three apps that will be included with Pixeom at launch are called File Exchange; Club Exchange; and Market Exchange — and they support, respectively, file-sharing &amp; document back-up, collaboration &amp; syncing across multiple devices; discussion boards for secure communication and digital socialising; and an interactive storefront for buying and selling, with Dwolla payment processing built-in.</p>
<p>Or, in other words, alternatives to stuff like Google Docs, Dropbox, Facebook, and Amazon Marketplace.</p>
<p><img alt="Pixeom screenshot" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-953113" height="376" loading="lazy" sizes="(max-width: 633px) 100vw, 633px" src="https://beta.techcrunch.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/screen-shot-2014-02-05-at-12-20-49.png" srcset="https://techcrunch.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/screen-shot-2014-02-05-at-12-20-49.png 633w, https://techcrunch.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/screen-shot-2014-02-05-at-12-20-49.png?resize=150,89 150w, https://techcrunch.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/screen-shot-2014-02-05-at-12-20-49.png?resize=300,178 300w, https://techcrunch.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/screen-shot-2014-02-05-at-12-20-49.png?resize=50,30 50w" width="633"/></p>
<p>There are also no subscription fees for using Pixeom or its apps. The business model relies solely on selling the hardware itself — at least for now. Sam Nagar tells TechCrunch that it may look for additional revenues streams in future.</p>
<p>“While we will make more than enough money off the sales of our devices to support ourselves (we are a very lean startup with high profit margins), we will later consider monetizing off of the Pixeom Exchange Network in more non-traditional ways,” he says.</p>
<p>“One of our ideas is if a device owner wanted their content to be discovered more easily, they can pay to have it show up higher on the listings, similar to how Google does it with their search engine, or how Facebook allows you to ‘sponsor’ a post.”</p>
<p>The Pixeom device is powered by a Raspberry Pi microcomputer — making it low power and helping to keep the cost down (early bird Kickstarter pledges for a $100 Pixeom are all gone but you can bag the device for $125). Each Pixeom has 16GB of internal storage, expandable via two USB ports to plug in your own hard drives.</p>
<p>Pixeom users can also choose for their device to stay private or invite trusted friends to collaborate within the secure network. There’s also an option to join a global network of Pixeom devices so that public content can be shared/advertised within that wider network.</p>
<p>The collaborative core of Pixeom’s platform means that a Pixeom owner can share content with anyone else (provided they have the ability to access a webpage). There’s no requirement for the content viewer to purchase Pixeom hardware or download their application, although there is a free mobile app for accessing the Pixeom Exchange Network of public content, as an alternative and richer point of entry to accessing this content via a web browser.</p>
<p>“The only time someone else needs to buy a Pixeom device is if they want to have their own private area that no one else can see, or if they want to start publishing their own public content on the Pixeom Exchange Network. Otherwise, a team of 25-100 people can all easily share and collaborate on one single device using their web browser,” adds Sam Nagar.</p>
<p><span style="line-height:1.625;">If all goes to plan Pixeom’s founders are aiming to ship to backers in May.  You can see the pair’s full Kickstarter pitch in the below video. </span></p>

<p>How many Pixeom’s do the founders reckon they can ship in the first year? “We project selling 400,000 – 500,000 units worldwide,” says Sam Nagar. “We are currently lining up channel partners and finalizing the designs for more types of hardware that our stack can run on which will appeal to a variety of users, and after talking with many of them as well as from our own research, we feel this is a fair estimate.”</p>
<p>The startup also has plans to expand the apps it offers in future — with cloud games one area it’s already working on — starting with “something like a trivia game or a more traditional one like chess/checkers”.</p>
<p><span style="line-height:1.625;">“People could have private games with each other, or match up with each other on the exchange network, all hosted on their devices. Their scores could be published and we could even give away benefits to the top public score of the day like promoting their content within the network. We want to be regularly involved with our user base and will continue to see how we can make using Pixeom regularly to become a fulfilling experience,” adds Nagar. </span></p>
<p>It’s also working on con conferencing and telepresence apps, everything from voice to screen sharing, he adds.</p>

The company\u2019s name is a mixture of \u201csalt,\u201d an important spice for cooking, and \u201ctalk,\u201d which he said \u201cis essential for our lives.\u201d<\/p>\n<p>Four years later, the company\u2019s 8,000-square-foot virtual kitchen is churning out orders from Saltalk\u2019s website, which features over 200 dishes.<\/p><div class=\"piano-inline-promo\"><\/div>\n<p>Virtual kitchens don\u2019t have dine-in facilities or the costs associated, which typically means they yield higher profit margins. The industry is hot right now and goes by other names, like dark kitchen, cloud kitchen or ghost kitchen, and involves having space in a central location where chefs whip up their culinary creations and have them delivered.<\/p>\n<p>The <a href=\"https:\/\/www.statista.com\/statistics\/1078732\/cloud-kitchen-market-size-us\/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">global virtual kitchen market<\/a> was valued at just over $43 billion in 2019 and is poised to grow to $71.4 billion by 2030, according to Statista figures. That compares to a trillion-dollar food industry.<\/p>\n<p>As the food delivery industry grew over the past 2 years, these types of kitchens also gained attention as we ate more from home during the pandemic. It not only provided restaurants a way to have online ordering capabilities without changing their own kitchens, but in some respects it also enabled them to experiment with small-batch cooking of some of their more popular menu items. In addition, when restaurants were laying off staff, it provided an outlet for chefs to supplement their income while creating their own menus.<\/p>\n<p>Investors were not far behind, pumping some $545 million of venture-backed funding into just the U.S.-based virtual kitchens in 2020, <a href=\"https:\/\/foodondemandnews.com\/02132020\/virtual-kitchen-investments-in-u-s-near-600-million\/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener\">according to Food On Demand<\/a>. is on a mission to provide online shoppers in Pakistan with \u201cAmazon-like\u201d next-day deliveries. The Karachi-based company announced it has raised $3.1 million in new funding from Y Combinatior, along with new investors i2i, Flexport, Soma Capital and Rebel Fund. Returning investors included GFC, Fatima Gobi and TPL E-ventures, along with Dropbox co-founder Arash Ferdowsi. This brings RIder\u2019s total raised to $5.4 million since September 2021.<\/p>\n<p>Founded in 2019 by former UPS Pakistan executive Salman Allana, Rider is building a network of sorting hubs, delivery centers and a digitized fleet. The platform enables sellers to offer next-day delivery with route optimization, live tracking and scheduling for buyers. The company claims that since their pre-seed investment round in September 2021, monthly revenues have grown 110% and they have doubled their customer base to 650 online sellers. So far, Rider has delivered 3 million parcels across 60 cities in Pakistan. It currently runs a network of 16 hubs that cover 60 cities across Pakistan, which Allana said accounts for about 60% of e-commerce demand in the country.<\/p>\n<p>Allana told TechCrunch that growing up in Karachi and spending his early career in sub-Saharan Africa meant he was used to poor supply chains and logistics services. \u201cIf you ordered something online, you accepted the huge risk it might never show up,\u201d he said. When he moved to London to study for his MBA, he became \u201cobsessed\u201d with Amazon delivery. \u201cHow could an order I placed at midnight be at my doorstep the next morning? I believed there was a clear and large opportunity to bring this service quality to online sellers in Pakistan and eradicate \u2018parcel anxiety\u2019 for all online buyers in Pakistan\u2014including myself.\u201d<\/p>\n<p>After earning his MBA, Allana started working for UPS Pakistan as head of strategy and business development. He saw for himself the challenges logistics incumbents face, including lost orders, buyers who are reluctant to order online again and, for online sellers, headaches like manual cash-on-delivery, reconciliation and slow payback, which created working capital challenges, especially for Pakistan\u2019s one million SMEs that rely on Instagram and Facebook to reach buyers.<\/p>\n<p>\u201cI learnt that the traditional delivery payers were not set up or equipped to service the online retail trend, and that change from the inside would be slow and costly,\u201d Allana said. \u201cThe COVID pandemic saw a huge and irreversible shift to online shopping across Pakistan. Only a built-for-purpose, dynamic, growth-focused startup could capture this opportunity on time.\u201d<\/p>\n<p>Logistics is a notoriously cash-burning sector. Allana said that the network of delivery centers Rider is building isn\u2019t what you would usually imagine. Instead they include mobile warehouses (or pre-sorted vans), empty spaces in the parking lot of malls and petrol stations. Moving forward, Rider would also like to have delivery centers in kiranas, or convenience stores. This means delivery centers are flexible enough to move as high volume e-commerce zones change.<\/p>\n<p>\u201cWe\u2019re fundamentally building for \u2018urban logistics,\u2019 so we don\u2019t have requirements for large sorting centers and spaces,\u201d he said. \u201cOur network consists of numerous small delivery centers which are purposely placed to cover high-volume e-commerce zones, and which ultimately are flexible to move as these zones change.\u201d<\/p><div class=\"piano-inline-promo\"><\/div>\n<p>Rider\u2019s new funding will be used on its in-house tech, including e-commerce enablement tools like plug-ins and built-in wallets to help SMEs, which Allana said are mostly owned by women, grow their businesses.<\/p>\n<p>\u201cOur ambition from day one\u2014we want to be the country\u2019s number end-to-end e-commerce logistics solution provider,\u201d Allana said. \u201cBut we see logistics as a series of building blocks, each of which we need to get right, operationally and financially, before we can build the next. Today, Rider is doing last-mile delivery to the customers\u2019 doorstep. We\u2019ve proven our last mile solutions work, we\u2019ve proven they work at scale and we now need to prove they work sustainably before we enter other verticals.\u201d He added Rider already has an eye on its next phase, and piloted its B2B movement, or overland trucking, in January.<\/p>\n<p>In a prepared statement, i2i general partner Kalsoom Lakhani said, \u201cAs the e-commerce industry in Pakistan grows, so will the need for a next generation 3PL player that understands the Pakistani market realities and knows how to build both aggressively but also efficiently. Is An Anti-Corporate Cloud Device That Wants To Give You Back Your Data Privacy"},"content":{"rendered":"<p id=\"speakable-summary\">Meet\u00a0<a target=\"_blank\" href=\"https:\/\/www.kickstarter.com\/projects\/pixeom\/pixeom-the-personal-exchange-device\">Pixeom<\/a>, a prototype device being Kickstarted by <span style=\"line-height:1.625;\">brother and sister duo Karishma and Sam Nagar, with the aim of offering alternative cloud services that don&#8217;t require you to hand over the keys to your private data in exchange for a convenient digital service. \u00a0<\/span><\/p>\n<p>Prior to Edward Snowden&#8217;s revelations about the <a href=\"https:\/\/beta.techcrunch.com\/tag\/nsa\/\">NSA<\/a>&#8216;s (and <a href=\"https:\/\/beta.techcrunch.com\/2013\/11\/01\/britains-gchq-collaborated-with-other-eu-nations-to-enable-broad-internet-surveillance\/\">other governments&#8217; security agencies<\/a>&#8216;) dragnet surveillance of everyone&#8217;s online lives, Pixeom would probably have sunk without a trace. Or not even been conceived of as a mainstream project at all.<\/p>\n<p>But, post-Snowden, there&#8217;s a perfect storm of <a href=\"https:\/\/beta.techcrunch.com\/2013\/07\/04\/spying-bad-for-business\/\">cloud-computing concerns<\/a> amassing to undermine the convenience of mainstream online services. And, after a month seeking crowdfunding on Kickstarter, Pixeom has passed its funding target of $50,000 &#8212; with close to 400 backers, and still has 15 days left to run on its campaign.<\/p>\n<p>In addition to the funds it&#8217;s currently raising on Kickstarter, Pixeom has also secured\u00a0more than $500,000 in private funding to get this personal cloud device to market.<\/p>\n<p>It&#8217;s clear that Snowden has shone a light on something very rotten lurking at the core of mainstream cloud services. Sure, web mail clients are handy &#8212; and you get a chunk of free storage &#8212; but you&#8217;re also effectively handing the keys to your inbox over to the NSA (et al) should they happen to feel like dipping into your personal correspondence one lunchtime or other.<\/p>\n<p>And of course it&#8217;s a given that your personal data will be mined by the service provider; it&#8217;s a free service, so your privacy is the product.\u00a0This general snooping on\/data-mining of users is a routine business model for the majority of free cloud-based services and apps.<\/p>\n<p>Multiple big name tech giants have built hugely lucrative businesses off of selling your likes and dislikes to advertisers in exchange for furnishing you with a platform where you can share that data in the first place. Or store the personal stuff they need to peek at to gather sellable intel on you.<\/p>\n<p>Plus there&#8217;s also the sloppy security of (<a href=\"https:\/\/beta.techcrunch.com\/2013\/12\/31\/hackers-claim-to-publish-list-of-4-6m-snapchat-usernames-and-numbers\/\">certain<\/a>) startups that leak your personal info all over the Internets just because they&#8217;re more interested in expanding their user-base than expending effort on adequate security measures.<\/p>\n<p>So it goes.<\/p>\n<p>Or so it has, but &#8212; whipped up by the storm of snooping revelations &#8212; some counter-currents are (finally) emerging to challenge the accepted wisdom that accessing the convenience of cloud services necessitates giving up your personal data and abandoning any shred of privacy in the process.<\/p>\n<p>Projects like <a href=\"https:\/\/beta.techcrunch.com\/2014\/01\/15\/blackphone\/\">Blackphone<\/a> or the <a href=\"https:\/\/beta.techcrunch.com\/2013\/11\/05\/dark-mail\/\">Dark Mail Alliance<\/a>. And now <a target=\"_blank\" href=\"https:\/\/www.kickstarter.com\/projects\/pixeom\/pixeom-the-personal-exchange-device\">Pixeom<\/a>.\u00a0Pixeom&#8217;s makers say they want to facilitate a new type of data exchange for Internet users which is hosted on an anti-corporate cloud that sits in your own home &#8212; i.e. not somewhere in a vast datacenter in California where giant branded spiders can crawl all over it.<\/p>\n<p>One half of the Pixeom founder team, Sam Nagar, who previously worked as a software engineer at Microsoft in Mountain View, describes the device as follows:<\/p>\n<blockquote><p><span style=\"color:#686c68;font-family:alright_sansmediumitalic, HelveticaNeue, Helvetica, Arial, sans-serif;line-height:1.5;\">In a nutshell:\u00a0<\/span><br \/>\n<span style=\"color:#686c68;font-family:alright_sansmediumitalic, HelveticaNeue, Helvetica, Arial, sans-serif;line-height:1.5;\">1. All of your cloud services in a box for a small one-time cost, we are a combination of Dropbox\/Box\/Facebook\/Shopify with a focus on privacy.\u00a0<\/span><\/p>\n<p><span style=\"color:#686c68;font-family:alright_sansmediumitalic, HelveticaNeue, Helvetica, Arial, sans-serif;line-height:1.5;\">2. Scalable device that continues to grow as one large storage pool instead of multiple &#8220;islands&#8221; of user data.<\/span><\/p>\n<p><span style=\"color:#686c68;font-family:alright_sansmediumitalic, HelveticaNeue, Helvetica, Arial, sans-serif;line-height:1.5;\">3. An optional exchange network that anyone can browse full of public content from people&#8217;s devices that can one day rival the large datacenter based public networks.<\/span><\/p><\/blockquote>\n<p>What exactly is Pixeom? It&#8217;s a personal, encrypted cloud device that plugs into your home Internet via Ethernet and provides cloud storage for your digital content without having to go through any third party intermediaries, and with support for unlimited expansion of said storage &#8212; either by chaining together multiple Pixeoms, or by plugging in additional USB hard drives.<\/p>\n<p>The device is not just focused on storage though. The Pixeom platform also offers alternative cloud apps to replace online services &#8212; name-checking the likes of Dropbox, Box, Google Drive, Microsoft Sharepoint, Facebook and Amazon as services it wants to offer an alternative to &#8212; in a simpler (and potentially cheaper) way than by having to set up a NAS box or host your own server.<\/p>\n<p>The three apps that will be included with Pixeom at launch are called File Exchange; Club Exchange; and Market Exchange &#8212; and they support, respectively, file-sharing &amp; document back-up, collaboration &amp; syncing across multiple devices; discussion boards for secure communication and digital socialising; and an interactive storefront for buying and selling, with Dwolla payment processing built-in.<\/p>\n<p>Or, in other words, alternatives to stuff like Google Docs, Dropbox, Facebook, and Amazon Marketplace.<\/p>\n<p><img loading=\"lazy\" class=\"alignnone size-full wp-image-953113\" alt=\"Pixeom screenshot\" src=\"https:\/\/beta.techcrunch.com\/wp-content\/uploads\/2014\/02\/screen-shot-2014-02-05-at-12-20-49.png\" width=\"633\" height=\"376\" srcset=\"https:\/\/techcrunch.com\/wp-content\/uploads\/2014\/02\/screen-shot-2014-02-05-at-12-20-49.png 633w, https:\/\/techcrunch.com\/wp-content\/uploads\/2014\/02\/screen-shot-2014-02-05-at-12-20-49.png?resize=150,89 150w, https:\/\/techcrunch.com\/wp-content\/uploads\/2014\/02\/screen-shot-2014-02-05-at-12-20-49.png?resize=300,178 300w, https:\/\/techcrunch.com\/wp-content\/uploads\/2014\/02\/screen-shot-2014-02-05-at-12-20-49.png?resize=50,30 50w\" sizes=\"(max-width: 633px) 100vw, 633px\" \/><\/p>\n<p>There are also no subscription fees for using Pixeom or its apps. The business model relies solely on selling the hardware itself &#8212; at least for now. Sam Nagar tells TechCrunch that it may look for additional revenues streams in future.<\/p>\n<p>&#8220;While we will make more than enough money off the sales of our devices to support ourselves (we are a very lean startup with high profit margins), we will later consider monetizing off of the Pixeom Exchange Network in more non-traditional ways,&#8221; he says.<\/p>\n<p>&#8220;One of our ideas is if a device owner wanted their content to be discovered more easily, they can pay to have it show up higher on the listings, similar to how Google does it with their search engine, or how Facebook allows you to &#8216;sponsor&#8217; a post.&#8221;<\/p>\n<p>The Pixeom device is powered by a Raspberry Pi microcomputer &#8212; making it low power and helping to keep the cost down (early bird Kickstarter pledges for a $100 Pixeom are all gone but you can bag the device for $125). Each Pixeom has 16GB of internal storage, expandable via two USB ports to plug in your own hard drives.<\/p>\n<p>Pixeom users can also choose for their device to stay private or invite trusted friends to collaborate within the secure network. There&#8217;s also an option to join a global network of Pixeom devices so that public content can be shared\/advertised within that wider network.<\/p>\n<p>The collaborative core of Pixeom&#8217;s platform means that a Pixeom owner can share content with anyone else (provided they have the ability to access a webpage). There&#8217;s no requirement for the content viewer to purchase Pixeom hardware or download their application, although there is a free mobile app for accessing the Pixeom Exchange Network of public content, as an alternative and richer point of entry to accessing this content via a web browser.<\/p>\n<p>&#8220;The only time someone else needs to buy a Pixeom device is if they want to have their own private area that no one else can see, or if they want to start publishing their own public content on the Pixeom Exchange Network. Otherwise, a team of 25-100 people can all easily share and collaborate on one single device using their web browser,&#8221; adds Sam Nagar.<\/p>\n<p><span style=\"line-height:1.625;\">If all goes to
